The invitation to run these workshops came from Hamza Ganić and Eldin Kurpejović, and I agreed right away. I said yes above all because, as it happens, I work online, so I could afford to be here in Rožaje and hold the workshops in person. In my opinion, that is the best way to pass on knowledge.

I am a software engineer with around eight years of experience in IT, a great lover of programming, of science in general, and especially of philosophy and film as an art form. I am often inclined to criticize those in power, society, or events in general, without having done much that is socially useful myself first. Because of that, an anecdote about the Scottish essayist Thomas Carlyle often echoes in my head. A young man once asked him how to go about reforming the world, and he replied: *"Reform yourself. That way there will be one less rascal in the world."* That is my starting point whenever I take on something serious in life. The combination of a love for knowledge, for programming, and a desire to change something in society led to the project **Youth for the Digital Promotion of Rožaje**.

## The goal wasn't just code

Hamza and Eldin gave me complete freedom to organize the sessions the way I thought was best. First and foremost, my goal was to teach them how knowledge is acquired, how to think, and how to rely on one's own capacities.

In my opinion, the lecture about how to learn and how to think was the most important of all. Technologies change, tools change, but the ability to think, to learn, to be patient and to arrive at solutions on your own — that stays.

![A student at the workshop](/images/blog/o-ruk-radionica/ucenik-rad.webp)

## The plan and the curriculum

There were twelve workshops in total. Roughly speaking, they were split so that the first few were a mix of theory and practice through hands-on examples in code, while the second half was dedicated to working on concrete projects in teams.

As for the material, we went through HTML, CSS, Git, GitHub and AI tools:

- **HTML and CSS** gave them the foundation to understand how a web page comes to be: how you build structure, how you add text, images, colors, spacing and the layout of elements.
- **Git and GitHub** showed them how serious work on code functions: how change history is kept, how you work in teams, how you create a branch, a Pull Request, a review and a merge.
- **AI tools** were a special topic, one I've given a dedicated section to below.

The biggest part wasn't in the slides but in working together. I wrote code live, made mistakes in front of them and fixed them out loud, because that process is exactly what's valuable — not the finished code.

![The group following code live on screen](/images/blog/o-ruk-radionica/grupa-kod.webp)

## Git and teamwork

It was especially important to me that Git not remain just a vague concept. We learned it from the terminal, step by step, until everyone had pushed their personal site and notes to their own GitHub profile. That way each of them walked away from the course with a real portfolio.

Then we moved on to what separates a hobby from serious work: working on a separate branch and submitting changes through a Pull Request. Everyone works on their own part, opens a Pull Request, and I review and merge the changes. That's how they felt what working in a team on a real project is like.

## AI as an amplifier of knowledge

We talked about AI tools in particular. My main message there was that AI is an excellent amplifier of our existing knowledge, much like money is an amplifier of our character traits.

If we know nothing, AI can often only help us make mistakes faster. But if we understand how things work, AI becomes a tool that speeds up the execution of what we already know.

![Presentation about artificial intelligence](/images/blog/o-ruk-radionica/ai-prezentacija.webp)

## The people who made the difference

The participants in this project are fantastic. Young, eager for knowledge, full of patience — and patience may be the single most important trait in programming. Programming very often means sitting for hours over a single problem, trying again and again, making mistakes, fixing them, then making mistakes again, until you finally arrive at a solution.

The students from the Vocational High School came in with really solid prior knowledge, so sincere praise goes to their teachers as well. The gymnasium students struggled a bit more at the start, but over time they achieved an excellent result and far greater ease in their work. That was perhaps the dearest thing for me to see: the moment when something that seemed hard at first suddenly becomes clear.

## Three teams, three real projects

In the second half, all that knowledge clicked into place. Three teams built three projects covering many topics about Rožaje, and I'm sure someone will benefit from each of them:

- **<a href="https://dozivirozaje.me" target="_blank" rel="noopener">dozivirozaje.me</a>**, a tourist guide through the town and its surroundings
- **<a href="https://guidetourrozaje.me" target="_blank" rel="noopener">guidetourrozaje.me</a>**, a guide to the tours and landmarks of Rožaje
- **<a href="https://skijalistestedim.me" target="_blank" rel="noopener">skijalistestedim.me</a>**, the Štedim ski resort

All of it was done while I followed the process through Pull Requests, in a very professional way. They went through branches, code review, merging changes and deploying the site to the internet. They felt all of it, from the very first recorded step to a site that is live and carries their name.

## Conclusion

![Presenting the project to the audience](/images/blog/o-ruk-radionica/prezentacija-publika.webp)

It's especially important to me that what they take away from these workshops isn't just knowledge of HTML, CSS, Git or GitHub. That's useful, of course. But it's far more important that they leave with the feeling that they can learn, that they can try, and that they can build something concrete. If even one of them thought, after these workshops, *"I can learn this too,"* then this project had meaning.

Thank you to Hamza and Eldin for the invitation and the trust, and thank you to these young people for showing that Rožaje has people who can do a great deal — they just sometimes need to be given the space, the time and the chance. That was the greatest reward for me.
